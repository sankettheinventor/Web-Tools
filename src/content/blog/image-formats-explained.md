Picking the wrong image format can mean blurry logos, bloated page weights, or photos that won't even open. Each format was built for a different job, and knowing which is which saves you time and storage. Here's a plain-English guide to the six formats you'll actually run into, what each is best for, and how to convert between them right in your browser.

## 1. JPG — Best for Photos

JPG (also written JPEG) is the workhorse of digital photography. It uses lossy compression, meaning it throws away some detail to keep file sizes small, which is a great trade-off for photos full of gradients and color. Avoid it for sharp-edged graphics, text, or screenshots, where the compression leaves visible smudges called artifacts. If a JPG is too large for email or a website, run it through the [Image Compressor](/tools/compress) to shrink it without re-shooting anything.

## 2. PNG — Best for Logos, Screenshots, and Transparency

PNG uses lossless compression, so it preserves every pixel exactly, and it supports a transparent background. That makes it the right pick for logos, icons, screenshots, and any graphic with crisp lines or text. The downside is file size: a photo saved as PNG can be several times larger than the same photo as JPG. Use PNG when sharpness or transparency matters, and switch to JPG when it doesn't.

## 3. WebP — Best for Fast-Loading Websites

WebP is a modern format from Google that does both jobs: it offers lossy and lossless modes, supports transparency, and typically produces smaller files than JPG or PNG at similar quality. That smaller size means faster page loads, which is why it's popular for websites. Browser support is now near-universal, but some older software and social platforms still don't accept it. If a site or app rejects a WebP, use the [Image Converter](/tools/convert) to turn it into a JPG or PNG.

## 4. HEIC — Best for Saving Space on iPhones

HEIC (High Efficiency Image Container) is Apple's default photo format, storing images at roughly half the size of JPG at comparable quality. It's excellent for keeping your phone's storage under control. The catch is compatibility: Windows, many Android devices, and plenty of websites still can't open HEIC files. When you need to share or upload one, convert it first with the [HEIC to JPG tool](/tools/heic-to-jpg), or set your iPhone to shoot in JPG under Settings, Camera, Formats.

## 5. SVG — Best for Logos and Icons That Scale

SVG is a vector format, which means it's stored as math rather than a grid of pixels. The result scales to any size without ever getting blurry, making it ideal for logos, icons, and illustrations that need to look crisp on everything from a favicon to a billboard. SVG files are also tiny when the artwork is simple. It's the wrong choice for photographs, though, since real-world images can't be described as clean shapes. If you need a small icon for your site, a [Favicon Generator](/tools/favicon) can produce the right sizes from a single image.

## 6. GIF — Best for Short, Simple Animations

GIF is the old standard for short looping animations and reaction clips. Its strength is broad support: GIFs play almost everywhere without a video player. The weakness is quality, because GIF is limited to 256 colors, so photos and detailed animations look banded and the files balloon in size. For modern animation, WebP or actual video formats are smaller and sharper, but GIF still wins when you need something that just works in any chat or email.
